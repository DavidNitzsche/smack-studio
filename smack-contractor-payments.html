Architecture
Stripe Connect account types
Three options. Only one makes sense for a studio management SaaS.
🏦
Standard
Contractor has their own full Stripe account. They manage everything themselves. Platform has minimal control. Built for independent businesses that already use Stripe — not for production studio contractors.

Wrong fit
⚡
Express — Use This
Stripe handles KYC/verification. Contractor sees a lightweight payout dashboard only. Platform controls all money flow, payout timing, and fees. ~2 min onboarding via a Stripe-hosted link. Perfect for "studio sends contractor a payment" use case.

Recommended
🔧
Custom
Platform builds everything — full white-label, no Stripe branding anywhere. Platform owns all compliance liability. Same fees as Express but 5× the engineering work. Revisit at significant scale if white-labeling becomes a competitive requirement.

Critical: Never use card-funded payouts If a studio funds their payout wallet via credit card instead of ACH, Stripe charges 2.9% + $0.30 on the funding transaction. $10,000 funded by card = $290 in fees before you've paid a single contractor. Require ACH for all contractor payment funding — no exceptions.
Critical: Don't transfer before ACH clears If a studio's ACH pull returns (insufficient funds) after you've already paid contractors, you've fronted the money. Use Stripe's source_transaction parameter to link transfers to the charge — transfers only execute when the charge clears.

Risk Management
Where you can lose money and how to stop it
The pricing model is only safe if these guardrails are built in from day one.
High-volume contractor on cheap tier
High risk
Studio has one contractor paid $80k/month on Starter ($19). Your Stripe cost: ~$202. Net loss: ~$183/month.
Enforce hard per-contractor volume cap in app. Block additional payouts once limit is hit. Show upgrade prompt.
Card-funded payouts
High risk
Studio funds $10k in contractor payments via credit card. Stripe charges 2.9% + $0.30 = $290+ before you've paid anyone. Completely wipes margin.
ACH bank account required for contractor payment funding. No card option presented in the UI. Period.
ACH return after payouts sent
Medium risk
Studio's bank pull bounces (insufficient funds) after contractors have already been paid. You've fronted the money. $4 return fee plus the full payout amount.
Never initiate contractor transfers until ACH has a charge.succeeded event. Use source_transaction to link. Funds stay pending 2–5 days.
International contractors
Medium risk
Cross-border payouts add 1–1.5% in FX fees. An $80k payout to a UK contractor costs ~$1,200 in FX on top of normal payout fees. Margin disappears fast.
US bank accounts only in V1. International is an Enterprise conversation with explicit FX pass-through or higher tier pricing.
Instant payout absorption
Medium risk
If you absorb the 1% instant payout fee, one $50k instant payout costs $500. Never absorb this — it doesn't scale at any volume.
Instant payouts are opt-in by contractor. Contractor pays a disclosed 1% fee. Standard payouts (free) are the default.
Volume at scale without negotiation
Long-term
At $500k+ annual payout volume, you're paying full Stripe list rates. At $1M+ those rates are negotiable — 20–40% off published prices is achievable.
Initiate Stripe volume pricing conversation at $2M+ annual Connect volume. Track this metric from launch.
Compliance
KYC, 1099s, and the DE 542
Stripe handles the heavy lifting. Your job is to make sure the right information is collected and the right boxes are checked in your Connect settings.
👷
What contractors must provide (Stripe collects this)
Full legal name · Date of birth · Home address · Last 4 of SSN (full SSN if auto-verification fails) · Bank account routing + account number

Business contractors (LLC, S-Corp): EIN · Business name · Business address · Representative identity + beneficial owners
🏢
What studios must provide (for payment funding)
Studios are your billing customers — they don't go through Connect KYC. They need:

Verified bank account via Plaid or Stripe's instant verification (for ACH debit) · Standard Smack subscription billing info

No Connect onboarding required for studios.
Compliance item Who handles it What you need to do
Contractor KYC / identity verification Stripe (via Express onboarding hosted link) Initiate onboarding via Account Links API. Stripe does the rest.
1099-NEC filing (federal) Stripe generates automatically Enable tax reporting in Connect Dashboard. Ensure contractors have valid TIN on file. $600 threshold triggers 1099.
1099-K (if applicable) Stripe generates automatically Same as above. IRS threshold currently $5,000 (2024–2025), further reductions planned.
California DE 542 You (platform) or the studio Report new independent contractors to CA EDD within 20 days of first payment over $600. One-time per employer-contractor relationship. See notes below.
AML / fraud monitoring Stripe (built-in for Express accounts) Stripe monitors connected accounts. Build a manual review queue for studios onboarding many contractors quickly.
On the California DE 542 The DE 542 is a one-time new contractor report per employer-contractor relationship. If a contractor was already set up in Gusto (or any other platform) by the same studio and a DE 542 was filed at that time, you do not need to re-file when they're added to Smack. The obligation is fulfilled. Only file DE 542 for contractors who have never been reported before by that studio. Build a checkbox in the contractor onboarding flow: "Already reported to CA EDD (e.g. via Gusto)?" — marks the DE 542 as satisfied without triggering a new filing.
History Reconciliation
Handling prior payments from other platforms
The $600 threshold for 1099-NEC is based on total annual payments from your business to that contractor — not per platform. If a contractor was paid $400 elsewhere and $300 in Smack, the combined $700 triggers a 1099. Reconciliation is required.
A
CSV Import
Export CSV from whatever platform the studio used before, import into Smack. Match on EIN/SSN first, legal name second, amount+date as fallback. Flag unmatched rows for manual review. More engineering, more that can go wrong with name format mismatches.
Chosen
B
Prior Payments Field
Single input per contractor: "Payments made outside Smack in 2026." Studio enters a number. Platform adds it to the running total for 1099 purposes. Optional notes field to log the source. Works regardless of what they used before — payroll software, checks, wire transfers, whatever.
C
Payroll Platform API
Pull payment history directly via API from whatever platform the studio used. No CSV export step. Reliable matching, cleanest UX. Significant engineering work and varies per platform. Phase 2 only if studios ask for it.
Option B is the right call for launch — for both Workprint and Smack One field per contractor. Works regardless of where prior payments came from. Zero parsing, zero matching logic, zero edge cases. The studio pulls the number from a bank statement or their old platform's report in 30 seconds. The 1099 filing checklist shows a breakdown ("$3,200 via Smack, $1,800 prior") for the studio's records but reports the combined $5,000 to the IRS.

What's relevant and what isn't

Stripe Treasury
Embedded studio financial accounts — studios maintain a running balance in the platform instead of pulling from their bank per-batch. Eliminates ACH funding fee. US only, limited availability, significant engineering lift. Evaluate at $1M+ ARR.
